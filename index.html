A Man, A Threat, and A Millon Books

Senn Hassan’s greatest obstacle in conceiving his most famous story, The Leningrad Affair , known as “The Leningrad Labyrinth” in some circles, revealed itself when he was coming up with a name for the protagonist. Like any master of craft would, Hassan intended to imbue the name of the character with symbolic value, while keeping the metaphor obscure enough so to not disclose the meaning to a reader without the reader having done at least some legwork. It was, according to all accounts — I have not read it — a sordid book, both in regard to its writing style and its subject matter. American critics called the book, “a pro-Communist ruse” and Soviet critics said it was “an inaccurate depiction of the Leningrad elite” and that, “he was misrepresenting their social connectedness.”

Hassan had never actually been to Leningrad, nor did he know anyone from Leningrad, which made it nearly impossible to write a factual story. But, as any Leningradian would remark, it actually is impossible to write a factual story about the Leningrad elite even for someone who intermingles with the coterie. It mutates too often. If one were to say that Anna Radinikov and Maria Lampov were cordially acquainted, then it might have been true at any point in the past and might be true at any point in the future, but neither of those temporal settings bears any correlation to the present setting of The Leningrad Affair. Which begs the more important question, is something true if only true for an instant? I suspect that is what Hassan intended. I know that, in the end, the publishing of The Leningrad Affair was a weight off his back. He was struggling with ideas bigger than himself at that time and once he published the book he went off the grid. No soul has seen him since, or no soul that I have talked to at least.

His disappearance in ’94 was under such curious circumstances that some figure it wasn’t a disappearance at all, and was rather a refashioning of his entire life. He’s probably living elsewhere under the name Satoshi Nakamoto, they say, no one had any reason to kidnap, maim, and kill him, they say, and he certainly had no reason to end his own life.

But I am sure he is dead.

I know because he once showed me a letter from a crazed admirer that said something vaguely resembling “your protagonist’s name must not immediately make sense to a reader for the safety of your own life.” Hassan kept this letter mostly secret, except for consulting me. He was terrified of the stalker, who had included their own interpretation of the origins of the character names for War & Fleas and Because of G.O.D, two of Hassan’s more obscure works. He felt that fate was slowly conspiring against him, turning his life into one of his own complex stories, where the name of a protagonist was the difference between life and death. Of the things that accounted for Hassan’s anguish in the year leading up to his disappearance – the year in which The Leningrad Affair was published -- the two most jarring were a) that the reader would discern the origins of his protagonist’s name (which would, for all intents and purposes, result in the termination of his existence) and b) the growing fear, because of the similarity between his own quandary and the convoluted plots of his books, that he was actually the main character in someone else’s book, in a layer above Hassan’s own consciousness. “I’m a puppet,” he told me once, “I’m a gosh darn puppet.”

He wrote to me in a letter just before he started writing The Leningrad Affair:

I’ve put in some thought and have concluded that the only way to escape my appalling destiny is to evade the admirer, the reader of my texts. It is clear that the reader has an unparalleled perspicacity and a determination to comprehend the most deep-seated of my symbols. I will not trick the reader, for the reader knows me better than I know myself as evidenced by the countless letters they have sent in which they analyzed the regrettable decisions of my youth and early adulthood and in which they questioned my devotion to my own mother. No single stone of my past would be left unturned and no single obscure story or myth will be left unconsidered. I am not fighting a he or she; I am fighting an ‘it’; an unstoppable force, a computer, or God himself. Therefore, I can only think of two ways to get out of the hopeless situation: the first is to kill myself, a cowardly move in the face of a powerful force. The second is to trap the reader in its own research, to lose the reader in a maze of sheer complexity from which it cannot escape, intertwining names and backstories and leads to the point where the reader becomes lost and confused and trapped in my labyrinth. This is my only hope. Let me know what you think of the plan.

Senn

I did not write back immediately, for he did not really care for my own approval, but I tracked his progress intimately throughout the months between the last letter I received and his disappearance. He published An Analysis of ‘Karpines’s Mirrors’, Tchemenon’s Last Myth through an auxiliary publishing company based in Jakarta, Indonesia that is tenuously associated with his own publishing company, and of which I am only aware of because he mentioned it to me in passing. He published a book titled The Discreet Librarian Guixong, written in Mandarin. He published seven more pieces, each published with invented dates: an anthology of Tchemenon’s myths; a History of the Library as a Philosophical Arena in Eastern Literature; a biography of Tang, the reclusive author of The Discreet Librarian, in which only a brief mention of The Discreet Librarian is made; a book about the most famous novelists of the Qing Dynasty, where only a quarter page is dedicated to Tang; a non-fiction book called The Secret Connection Between the Nazis and Tchemenon; a pioneering effort on puzzle theory in which Hassan argues that the only way to get to the bottom a mystery is to try every possibility, otherwise known as the “brute force method”; and finally, an English translation of The Discreet Librarian Guixong. He also conducted a fake interview where he told the interviewer -- whom I am certain was also Hassan himself because he used the word “flummox” -- that he was vacillating between two names for the protagonist of his next book, The Leningrad Affair. Those two names, he said, were Karpinov and Guinov, and I had a feeling I knew their origins.

From An Analysis of ‘Karpines’ Mirrors’, Tchemenon’s Last Myth:

There are more reasons to be skeptical of the authenticity of Tchemenon’s last myth than just the faux-Greek name Karpines. Everywhere throughout Karpines’ Mirrors there are anachronisms casting doubt in the minds of classics scholars. Critics have suggested that Tchemenon did not write the myth and, like Homer, it was actually a committee, but not a committee of Tchemenon’s contemporaries, rather a committee of modern writers trying to forge their way into history.

Nowhere in other ancient Greek literature can one find a mention of Karpines’ Mirrors. Mentions of his other works appear frequently in Herodotus’s writings, giving them historical legitimacy. However, the same level of historical legitimacy cannot be claimed about Karpines’ Mirrors, as no scholar has ever come across a mention of it in legitimized literature. That suggests two possibilities, that Karpines’ Mirrors was disliked by the Greeks, who loved the rest of Tchemenon’s myths, or that the Greeks were unaware of it, giving promise to the theory that the myth was created hundreds of years later.

That is purely speculation, though, and offers no conclusive evidence that Karpines’ Mirrors are a farce. But Karpines’ Mirrors plot is incongruous with the styles and themes of Tchmenon’s other plots. The concept of the mirrored maze that serves as a vehicle for Karpines’ self-discovery is too emotionally nuanced to have been the work of Tchemenon, a man whose myths are normally full of emotionless action.

In addition, Greek myth usually feature Gods or Goddesses as the protagonist, but Karpines’ Mirrors features Karpines, who is a mortal. A God is only present when Karpines imagines Daedalus somewhere in the infinity extending between two parallel mirrors. When he speaks to Daedalus, the maker of the mirrored labyrinth, Karpines asks which way to proceed, as Karpines himself is highly disoriented. Daedalus disappears and it is then that Karpines understands that the way out of the labyrinth is not to ask for assistance, but rather skirt along the walls and cut the bottom of his foot to leave a trail. If Tchemenon wrote this myth, Daedalus would’ve helped Karpines exit the labyrinth. As it is, the solution is too humanist for Tchemenon to have written it. He was a firm believer in the hopelessness of man’s condition, as noted in Themenoclys’s biography of Tchemenon. As Themenoclys writes, “In a room with Tchemenon, you never get the feeling he trusts himself or anyone else. Yet his misanthropy is simply another sign of his piety. He prays to the Gods ten times a day and hopes that his prayers will make the Gods shine their favor upon him” [translation]. If Tchemenon was a misanthrope, would his last myth feature a man finding his own way without the help of a God? Maybe he had a change of heart, but it seems unlikely.

The absolutely damning evidence that Karpines’ Mirrors was not written by Tchemenon comes in the form of diction, specifically the Greek word “ethelos.” This word, found just two paragraphs from the end of the myth, is the proverbial “nail in the coffin” on the Karpines’ Mirrors debate. “Ethelos,” translating roughly into irrigation system, was not actually a word in the Greek lexicon until long after Tchemenon lived, meaning he couldn’t have possibly used this word in his myth. The story goes that the Greek word “ethelos” was put into Greek vocabulary after the Roman implementation of Aqueducts, which Greeks in the Roman period had no word for. Therefore, “ethelos” would still show up in a dictionary of ancient Greek words, but it would have never been used by Tchemenon who predated the word by about 400 years. This means that, at the earliest, this myth could’ve been written around 300 BC. However, it could be reasonably argued that Karpines’ Mirrors was written after the publication of the bestselling “Ancient Greek Dictionary for Modern Writers” because the dictionary’s imperfect translation of “ethelos” to mean “unexpected river or stream” is exactly how “ethelos” is used in Karpines’ Mirrors. The translation of Karpines’ Mirrors gives the contested line as:

"An unexpected stream drifted down from Karpines’ eyes, touched his cheek, and dripped off his chin, floating to the floor."

Whereas a correct translation of “ethelos” would warrant the result:

"An irrigation system drifted down from Karpines’ eyes, touched his cheek, and dripped off his chin, floating to the floor."

If the theory is correct, this small inconsistency in the etymology of ethelos dates the word to the 20th Century. Deciphering exactly who penned Karpines’ Mirrors would be undoubtedly difficult, but one curious element of the mass market paperback edition give historians pause: the specific blend of paper used in the book, a trademark of Down Under Publishing, was used frequently by the WHIFF collective to publish.

A Critical Analysis of Ryoku’s recursive building, by Takashi Makioka

Some have called Ryoku’s Tokyo Center for Future Design “a triumph of architectural thought” or “the product of an unparalleled wit,” but I reject those claims – adamantly might I add. Ryoku’s Center for Future Design is an asinine attempt at “boundary pushing,” a holy-grail ideal, which “the proprietors of contemporary architecture” shamelessly promote in order to keep themselves afloat upon the “slowly sinking water recliner” of faux-intellectual “taste making.”

The entire premise is founded upon this “holy” idea known as recursion – a something inside of a something, outside of a something else, layered both up down into the hissing nether regions of infinity. Escher played with recursion. That doesn’t mean Ryoku should, too. The funny thing about oblong obsessions with recursion… “the funny thing”… is that it doesn’t transfer well to the physical world, especially the manmade world, as it becomes not an intellectual game, but an exercise in static repetition. As we hear cupping noises opening the Matryoshka doll, we are not impressed that there are more dolls inside, but rather wonder how far down it will go and, more importantly, when it will end.

Such is how one must interpret Ryoku’s Tokyo Center for Future Design. We are not impressed that he has built miniature models of an inconsequential building, nor fooled by the implication that layers exist beyond the outer wall, as we can step outside and look upon the horizon, with the same worldview that we had when we entered the building, and see that there is no partition. And as for the claim that an infinite square footage has been created — measure Theory disproved that long ago.

The WHIFF collective, known rather leadingly as the Writers for Historical Information Freedom and Fodder, is a nefarious group whose objective is to subtly rewrite history through the means of forgery and falsification. Their title has often been mistaken to suggest that they are a group concerned with the ethical implications of having some history privatized. But their aim is to not make history equally available to all – rather, in fact, their aim is to make “true” history available to only them by rewriting and being able to distinguish what is falsified and what exactly happened.

The formation of this collective is widely considered, among scholars who are aware of WHIFF’s existence, to be a rejection of the philosopher Adatumbe Kuneyemi’s famous statement that “History is stone-like.” That would date WHIFF as having been formed no earlier than 1958, however it’s hard to say when, truly, WHIFF was most active, as their contributions to history are numerous, diverse, obscure, and intentionally obfuscated.

[…]

Considered to be one of their most infamous “stunts” – how else may we classify this type of deeply irresponsible intrusion in history? -- among those who are aware of WHIFF, is the false biography of Lyndon B Johnson told through the lens of an assistant who did not exist, David Boyd. The work, called Lyndon, under one of WHIFF’s many pennames, “David Foster Wallace,” is regarded as the de facto biography of Lyndon B Johnson, who named his own penis Jumbo and whose wife was referred to as Lady Bird, despite painting him in a light that conflicted what his closest associates remembered of him, recounted in another biography, Lyndon Baines Johnson and the Great Society. However, those close associates largely admitted that Lyndon was hard to biographize; rather than being boringly static, Lyndon Baines was erratic, unpredictable, equally likely to berate and hug in any given instant. The trouble with writing a biography of Lyndon B Johnson, they said, was that he encapsulated a broader spectrum of humanity than could reasonably be captured in a few-hundred page book – at once tortured and exalted, kind and cruel, hopeful for democracy yet dictatorial.

WHIFF probably chose Johnson for that exact reason – the biographer ultimately had to realize the subjectivity of the biography, that such words spilt on Lyndon couldn’t be classified as truths, only situated truths moderated by structural relationships he engaged in. Johnson is one example, a “larger than life figure,” but to what degree can this be extended to any person? Can an individual be wrapped up into a finite sum of words?

Before Freud: Marcotto Bruni and the Menefreghistari

Despite the Italian Risorgimento, ordering Italy into a proper nation-state like much of Europe was doing at the time after the Napoleonic rampage in the early years of the 19th Century, the Italian government still did not have full jurisdiction in one miniscule village, entirely walled, along the North Eastern boundary of Italy, as defined by the new Italian government. North East of Venice by a day or two, the amount of hours depending on the mode of transportation, as a horse-drawn carriage could take several days due to the relative likelihood of planting an oversized wooden wheel in a ditch, “attenti al fosso,” as the Italians said, “cave fossam,” as the Romans said, leaving horseback as the only legitimate option to thread through the susurrous lower Alps and even so it wasn’t particularly popular among Venetian aristocrats and the Venetian underclass didn’t have access to horses meaning it was all but disconnected from any major hub of cosmopolitanism, the village of Tarvisio operated under an entirely different paradigm than the rest of the country.

Their isolation from the rest of developed intellectual Italy lead the people in Tarvisio to eschew Enlightment reason and instead leave themselves vulnerable to alternative forms of “knowing” about the world. In those years, before the Italy army did finally take control over the village, Tarvisio was run by a man named Marcotto Bruni, who was ushering in a new age of cognitive theory through the ongoing and sometimes forceful research of dreams and the subconscious. He, backed by a group of broad-shouldered ruffians called the Menefreghistari, dominated the townspeople using a dream-inducing Serum called Mitrocessa, whose ingredients included fig tree root, but only a pinch, with the other ingredients being mostly unknown despite vast speculation, teeth, goat’s blood, goat’s milk (Marcotto Bruni himself consistently herded 12 billy on his farm, and when an addition was born, he slaughtered the oldest, enough to raise suspicion). Marcotta ushered in an age of total subservience under the policies of his research. Men and women were split into two camps, separated by the main road of Tarvisio, a dirt road that was patrolled constantly by the Menefreghistari, and only on the second and fourth Thursdays of the month were the men and women rejoined, precisely, as Marcotto Bruni said, “for the creation of somnofreghri,” which was one of Bruni’s many neologisms and whose exact meaning is hard to pin down but likely translates generally to “sleep slave.” Bored women used looms to make exquisite textiles, while men sowed the fields, with equal disinterest in their work. It’s not that it was boring work necessarily, its that within those 31-foot 3-inch walls, a figure the Menefreghistari consistently remind the citizens of – “how do you plan to get down?” they ask, “you’re not going to jump are you?” and laugh, knowing the futility of the Travisian adulthood domesticity, and their own futility – to incite fear, within those the Travisian walls, the Eastern age-paradigm was entirely reversed: a person had passed the pinnacle of their life by the age of 12 by Brunian Tarvisian standards, and life beyond that moment was suffering and worthlessness.

That phenomenon occurred mostly due to the exciting life that children led in their early years, separated from their parents working slave labor, they also working slave labor unknowing, strangers, in their own camp up the hill from Tarvisio. Inside the camp, in the plushest beds the Menefreghistari could import set on bed frames that never creaked, in rooms all to their selves, the children were taught to dream vividly throughout the night (aided of course by Bruni’s serum, which occasionally made children dream while wide awake). After night’s sleep, children were asked to write down their dreams otherwise meals were withheld from them. During the day time, each child was taught extensively, learning how to read well by age 4, when a child could start training in his or her assigned subject, ranging from philosophy to mythology to political theory to biology to literature. By 8 the children had the reading level of a college-educated adult, and by 12 they were prodigal experts in their own field after years of reading and considering seminal texts, one per day. But the high-levels of education were only a by-product of Marcotto Bruni’s wishes to study the effects of different types of thought processes and education on dreams. And so, when the children were ousted from the camp on the hill, their clothes stolen off their back, wearing nothing, cast down the hill to Tarvisio on the eve of their 13th birthday, their intellectual desires could no longer be satisfied, condemned to a life of solemn domesticity, of pastures and woven textiles.

But from the effort of the child dreamers, a great body of work arose for Marcotto Bruni, thousands of recorded dreams, changing the variables every night – who is going to bed later tonight? who is getting less to drink? who is getting riled up before bed? The accumulation of all these dreams, he thought, could point him in the direction of God, because God interacted with humans through the dream-world. In 18871, Bruni won a Menzione Distinta (Distinguished Mention) prize for his 20-plus years of research from the Roman Catholic Church, which he kept on his desk alongside the recorded dream he found most beautiful in all his years of dream research and a book by historian Vicenzo Abrutti detailing the Marxist uprising that occured in the camp in Bruni’s early years ruling Tarvisio. The writer of Bruni’s most treasured dream-record, ironically, was also the ringleader of the uprising in Tarvisio.

From The First Marxist Uprising: Tarvisio, Dream Mining, and the Lost Intellectuality of a Generation:

In the late 1800s the adult population of a small walled village, which was largely cut off from the rest of society, in Italy called Tarvisio suffered from an extreme malaise towards the structure of their society. That’s because an oppressive regime ruled by the dream-researching tyrant Marcotto Bruni fostered prodigal intellectual development among the youth, separated entirely from their parents, of Tarvisio in order to study the relationship between different types of thought processes, the dream-world, and the subconscious. He is widely considered one of the earliest and most influential scholars on the topics of the subconscious, specifically on the symbolism of dreams. Sigmund Freud is known to have visited Tarvisio in the early 1880s.

When children turned 13, though, they were cast out from the calculated, intellectualized excess of their youth into a life of simple domesticity in Tarvisio, forced to leave behind every single one of the thousand or so books each child had read by the eve of their departure. The remainder of their lives was laborious and loveless. Tarvisian adults worked long hours working the fields for food as a man, and weaving fine textiles as a woman. The men’s and women’s camps were entirely divided from one another, their only time to convene for a few hours on Saturday nights, during which procreation was expected by the Menefreghistari, Bruni’s order-keeping henchmen, in order to reproduce the social structure. When a child was born, that child was taken from the mother directly to the camp up the hill where the dream mining took place. Overqualified for and unsatisfied by their labor, the adults were left to complete the dreariest tasks at the bottom of the hill with the pinnacle of their lives already behind them. What kept them from suicide was the thought that their labor was the only thing keeping their children alive up the hill in the camp that the Tarvisians called, “the dream machine.”

It wasn’t until a young girl named Camilla Musacchio came down the hill from the camp fresh off her studies of Marxist theory. Her studies taught her to recognize exploitative labor relations, which were abundantly obvious in Tarvisio. She, through the force of her will and the naivete of her youth, revitalized an adult population that had long since ceased caring about their suffering, accepting it as a fact of life.

“It doesn’t have to be this way,” she would urge in the pipsqueak tongue of a 13-year old girl, “You don’t have to be the means to someone else’s end.”

She, now the de facto leader of an anti-authoritarian labor movement, to be – finally -- beneficiaries of their own labor, first devised to build a system of tunnels underneath the two camps so that they could collaborate more frequently on plans to restructure their village. The Tarvisians had a man from a nearby town, who would drop off entertainment (chess boards and the like) as well as tidbits of news from Venice which shot through Tarvisio like a wildfire. Camilla asked him to bring shovels, one every time he visited until they had over 20 shovels total. Time was not an object in the camp. They were living out their death sentence of oppressive mundanity. Any excitement was welcomed.

By Camilla’s 15th birthday, the Travisians had completed an underground system of sanctuaries connecting both the men’s and women’s camps, the entrance on the women’s side hidden beneath Camilla’s cabinet. She had committed her non-working hours entirely to the construction of the tunnel, but in the process she had lost her reason for doing so. The expansion of the tunnel consumed her thoughts and derailed, for a time, any hope of a Marxist uprising within Tarvisio.

In the meantime, she had built a surreptitious architectural under the pastures within the walls of the village. The tunnels were labrynthine and extending in all directions. This, as Camilla wrote in her journal, was meant to protect Travisians if there ever were to be an invasion: they could evade the Menefreghistari by sheer knowledge of the maze-like system. The system was never able to eclipse the edge of the village, though, with the wall foundations being, Camilla said, surprisingly deep. It was during this time that Camilla reacquired a book collection (no longer confined to Marx and other similar writings), which she stored below ground. It would only be logical for me to admit, despite my prolonged doubts, that Senn has eluded even I, who, curious, seek, like the superfan, a thorough understanding of the names of Senn’s protagonists. Senn’s wit has proven impenetrable at times and debilitating at others; his ability to take on other voices and to construct other worlds is beyond logical comprehension.

Each one of his works begged me to read the next; I could only understand the book that way, by immersing myself, a friend of his, in both what comes before a given piece as well as what comes after, the way discrete histories blend into one another, a person unable to conceptualize something without the concrete knowledge of what came before as well as what came after. That, I have concluded, is true not just for imaginary texts, with their ease of creation and destruction and their impossible flexibility, but life, too, in its concrete realness. I wrote a letter to Senn, with whom I assumed was dead by the time of my penning, extinguished by the admirer, who likely bested him in the years I’ve been reading the web of his works, explaining such thoughts about his web of works, and his admirer, and even my own life:

Senn,

I’ve now spent a few years of my retirement tracking the work that you intended to keep secret, only for the admirer to find. But, I must say, I have been curious, about you, about this ever-expansive web of worlds you have built, and about the admirer. I read the first few of your works which sent me straight down the rabbit-hole, which has in turn cost me years of my life, years of research that I could have spent writing my own book, or preparing my death bed, or being present for my granddaughter’s adolescence, 15 now, but instead I have spent time playing the game that was not designed for me, mapping out the connections between books on a wall in my home: I simply refused to let the meaning of one book pass through my fingers, for I wanted to know how it all ended, where the web came to a definitive end and if I could find that end, I would know that you, who I care most dearly about, had met your maker and that the admirer had sent you to him. But in the process, your works have taught me the most wonderful thing about life, a fact that I could have never reached upon my own reflection. I realize now that life is no isolated incident, that at the beginning of life you are born until one day you are dead, instead life is an ongoing process of transformation that you as an individual move through, we are the moving pieces meant to replenish the social structure until we don’t fit in the social structure anymore, but that may seem like a pessimistic outlook, yet I don’t consider it so: we are the connectors of what came before us to what came after us, and our lives make a profound influence in how those before and after are connected. Without us, our generation, any generation, the connection would sever. We are the continuous flow of the universe, indistinguishable from and totally interrelated with the past and the future, living on forever through our web of influences.

It makes me think about my mother, from whom I ran away and abandoned at age 14. I viewed her as restrictive to my life. I wanted to go away to a big city, and I did -- San Francisco – but left her completely in my wake, in Modesto, California. Why did I believe that my life was so untethered from hers that I could simply run away? I saw my life as an isolated incident whose potential I had to maximize. Oh, Senn! What was I thinking? She was heartbroken! I pray that you are better to your kin than I was to mine, for the sake of our universe – your mother Guyan and your daughter Karpenna – but I suspect that you have been good to them, for you would not have had so much trouble choosing the name of your protagonist if you preferred – the universe would admonish you! -- one to the other.

Your friend, there, waiting for you somewhere on the other end, wherever that is,

JLB
